The CAGED System
Foundation · Fretboard Navigation

CAGED is a framework for understanding the entire guitar fretboard as five overlapping chord shapes. The name comes from the five open chord forms: C, A, G, E, and D. Every major chord, scale, and arpeggio can be mapped to one of these five positions.

The key insight is that these shapes chain together in a fixed sequence. Play a C shape, and the next shape up the neck is always A, then G, then E, then D — then back to C one octave higher.

Why it matters

Without CAGED, most guitarists learn chord shapes in isolation. CAGED connects those dots — once you know the system, you can play any chord in any position without memorising hundreds of individual shapes. It also gives you a common language for scales, arpeggios, and lead playing.

How to practise it

Step 1. Pick one key — G major is a good start. Find all five G major barre chord shapes up the neck. Make sure they all sound like G.

Step 2. Add the major scale pattern over each shape. You're learning one scale in five positions, not five separate scales.

Step 3. Add the arpeggio (chord tones: 1–3–5) over each shape. This bridges rhythm and lead playing.

Step 4. Solo over a backing track, consciously moving from shape to shape rather than staying in one box.

The 5 CAGED Shapes
CAGED · Chord Shapes · Fretboard

Each shape is named after the open chord it resembles. In the key of C the shapes fall naturally at open position. Move to another key and the shapes shift — but their internal geometry is identical.

C Shape

Root on the B string (2nd string). Often played as a partial voicing. As a scale position it covers the middle of the neck comfortably. The hardest shape to barre cleanly.

A Shape

Root on the A string (5th string). One of the most useful barre chord shapes — the standard A barre at fret 2 gives you B major. Arpeggio runs very naturally from low to high string.

G Shape

Root on strings 6 and 1. Spans a wide stretch of four to five frets. Often played with partial voicings or as a cowboy chord in open position.

E Shape

Root on the low E string (6th). The standard E barre chord — move it to fret 3 and you have G, fret 5 is A, and so on. The most widely used barre chord shape on the guitar.

D Shape

Root on the D string (4th). Usually played as a partial chord using only strings 1–4. Creates a bright, high voicing. Requires muting the low strings when used as a barre.

Minor voicings

Every CAGED shape has a minor version — lower the major third by one fret. The shapes keep the same geometry; only one note changes per string. This applies identically to all five shapes.

CAGED Arpeggios
CAGED · Arpeggios · Lead Guitar

An arpeggio is the notes of a chord played one at a time. CAGED maps five arpeggio patterns across the neck — one per shape. This is the bridge between rhythm and lead playing.

Major arpeggio tones: 1 – 3 – 5

In G major: G, B, D. Each CAGED shape contains these three note types. When soloing, landing on these tones — especially on strong beats — sounds musical over any major chord.

How to practise
1. One shape, clean
Play one arpeggio shape ascending and descending. Every note clear. Start at 60 BPM.
2. Connect shapes
Play shape 1, shift to shape 2 without stopping. Aim for seamless hand-position changes.
3. Over backing
I chord backing track. Solo using only chord tones. Land on roots and 3rds on beat 1.
4. ii – V – I
Match arpeggio to each chord: Am arpeggio → D arpeggio → G arpeggio. This is jazz foundation.
Sweep picking connection

Sweep picking patterns are almost always CAGED arpeggio shapes. The E-shape 3-string minor arpeggio and the A-shape 5-string major arpeggio are the two most common starting targets.

Pentatonic Overlay
CAGED · Pentatonic · Lead Guitar

The five pentatonic boxes map directly onto the five CAGED shapes. Position 1 (the classic box) overlays the E shape. Position 2 overlays the D shape, and so on. Once you know this, you always know where your pentatonic box is from a chord shape.

Minor vs major pentatonic

Minor pentatonic: 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7. Major pentatonic: 1 2 3 5 6. They share the same five physical patterns — only the root changes. A minor pentatonic = C major pentatonic. Same fingerings, different tonal centre.

The blues note

Add a ♭5 to the minor pentatonic: 1 ♭3 4 ♭5 5 ♭7. This "blue note" creates the characteristic tension of blues and rock. In each box there is one natural location per string to insert it.

The Major Scale
Scales · Foundation · All Styles

The major scale is the foundation of Western music theory. All intervals, chord names, and mode names are defined relative to it.

Formula: W W H W W W H

Starting from C: C D E F G A B C. The gaps between E–F and B–C are half steps (one fret). All others are whole steps (two frets).

On the guitar

The major scale has five fingering patterns based on CAGED. Each covers two octaves. The most common starting points are the 6th string root pattern (E shape) and the 5th string root pattern (A shape). Know both of these fluently before studying modes.

The Natural Minor Scale
Scales · Rock · Blues · Metal

The natural minor scale (Aeolian mode) is the most common scale in rock, metal, and blues. Formula: W H W W H W W. Starting from A: A B C D E F G A.

A natural minor uses the same notes as C major — it's the 6th mode of the major scale, starting on the 6th degree.

DegreeMajorNatural MinorDifference
33♭3Lowered — defines minor quality
66♭6Lowered
77♭7Lowered
Harmonic minor

Raises the ♭7 to a natural 7, creating a strong pull to the tonic and an exotic "classical" sound. Used heavily in metal, neo-classical, and Spanish flamenco.

Melodic minor

Raises ♭6 and ♭7 when ascending, reverts to natural minor descending. Common in jazz. The ascending form sounds like major with a ♭3.

Pentatonic Scales
Scales · Lead Guitar · Most Used

The pentatonic removes two notes from the major or minor scale, leaving five notes that work over almost any chord in the key. It's forgiving, intuitive, and the backbone of rock, blues, and country lead.

Minor Pentatonic: 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7

In A: A C D E G. Works over Am, A7, and dominant chords in blues. Five box patterns cover the entire neck.

Major Pentatonic: 1 2 3 5 6

In G: G A B D E. Brighter and more resolved. Common in country, gospel, and classic rock.

Relative relationship

A minor pentatonic = C major pentatonic. Same notes, same fingerings, different root. You can shift between these feelings over the same shape by changing your target note.

Expression techniques
Bends
Bend the 4 up to 5, or ♭7 up to 1. Most expressive moves in minor pentatonic.
Vibrato
Apply to sustained notes — root, ♭3, and 5 especially. Width and speed define your voice.
Slides
Slide into the root from a half step below. Instant bluesy phrasing.
Double stops
Play 3rd and 5th, or ♭3 and ♭7 simultaneously. Big sound, minimal movement.
The 7 Modes
Scales · Advanced · Colour & Texture

Modes are variations of the major scale, each starting on a different degree. They share all the same notes as their parent major scale but have a different tonal centre — which creates a completely different mood.

Characteristic tones

Each mode has one note that defines its character. Dorian: natural ♮6. Lydian: ♯4. Mixolydian: ♭7. Phrygian: ♭2. Emphasise these over the matching chord to make the mode sound intentional rather than accidental.

Learn in this order

Ionian → Aeolian → Dorian → Mixolydian → Phrygian → Lydian → Locrian. Learn Dorian early — it's in more classic rock and jazz songs than any other minor mode.

Intervals
Chord Theory · Foundation

An interval is the distance between two notes in semitones. All chords and scales are built from stacked intervals. Knowing intervals lets you build any chord from scratch.

On guitar

Every fret on the same string is one semitone. The same interval has the same shape on adjacent strings — with one exception: the G to B string pair is one semitone wider than all other adjacent pairs.

Triads
Chord Theory · Building Blocks

A triad is three notes — root, third, fifth. Every chord in Western music is an elaboration of a triad. There are four types:

Inversions
Root position
1-3-5 from bottom. Most stable and grounded.
1st inversion
3-5-1 from bottom. Lighter. Used for smooth bass movement.
2nd inversion
5-1-3 from bottom. Creates harmonic tension. Common in classical cadences.
Triads on guitar

Most practical on three adjacent strings. The most useful sets are strings 1–2–3 and strings 2–3–4. These moveable three-string shapes are more versatile than full open chords for rhythm and lead work.

The tritone substitution

In any dominant 7th chord the 3rd and ♭7 form a tritone. This tritone is shared by the dominant 7th chord a tritone away — meaning G7 and D♭7 are interchangeable in many contexts. The basis of tritone substitution in jazz.

Extensions: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths
Chord Theory · Jazz · Advanced Voicing

Extensions continue stacking thirds above the 7th. The 9th is the 2nd an octave up; the 11th is the 4th; the 13th is the 6th. They add colour without changing the chord's fundamental function.

Shell voicings on guitar

You can't play a full 13th chord — that's 7 notes on 6 strings. Use shell voicings: root + 3rd + 7th + one extension. Drop the 5th first. A jazz guitarist playing Cmaj9 typically plays root, 3rd, 7th, 9th — four notes total.

The Hendrix chord

Dominant 7♯9 stacks a ♯9 (enharmonically ♭3) on top of the dominant 7th — creating simultaneous major and minor sound. The open E7♯9 voicing Hendrix used on Purple Haze is the most famous example in rock history.

Chord Quality Recognition
Ear Training · Harmony

Recognising chord quality by ear — major, minor, dominant, diminished — is a core musicianship skill. Each type has a distinct emotional character.

Major
Bright, stable, happy. The major third on top is the defining sound. Think: sunrise.
Minor
Dark, introspective. The flattened third changes everything. Think: rainy day.
Dominant 7th
Tense, bluesy. The tritone between 3rd and ♭7 creates urgency. Think: leaning forward.
Diminished
Spooky, maximally tense. All minor thirds stacked. Think: horror film.
Major 7th
Lush, dreamy. The major 7th adds warmth without tension. Think: late night jazz.
Suspended
Open, unresolved. No third at all — neither major nor minor. Think: held breath.
Training approach

Start by distinguishing major from minor — this covers 80% of music. Then add dominant 7th vs major 7th. Then diminished. Use the drone in this app: play a drone, then play a chord and sing the third. Major or minor third? That single question is the foundation of all chord quality recognition.

Common Progressions by Ear
Ear Training · Progressions · Practical

Most popular music uses a small number of progressions. Recognising them by ear lets you learn songs faster, jam without charts, and understand what makes music feel the way it does.

The key skill: follow the bass first

Train your ear to follow the bass note first, not the chord voicing. Bass movement tells you the progression. From there, determine whether each chord is major or minor.

ProgressionKey of GFeeling
I – IV – V – IG – C – D – GComplete, resolved. Blues, rock, country.
I – V – vi – IVG – D – Em – CUplifting. Major key pop.
vi – IV – I – VEm – C – G – DEmotional. Same notes, minor feel.
i – VII – VI – VIIAm – G – F – GDark, driving. Rock ballads, metal.
ii – V – IAm7 – D7 – Gmaj7Resolution. Jazz, sophisticated pop.
I – ♭VII – IVG – F – CAnthemic. Classic rock, Mixolydian feel.
The one-chord drill

Put on a song and identify just the first chord. Is it major or minor? Is it the tonic? Do this for five minutes daily and your ears will develop faster than any other method.
